Asthma - Symptoms, Triggers, and Treatment

Asthma is a chronic respiratory condition in which the airways become inflamed, narrowed, and produce extra mucus, making it difficult to breathe. It affects people of all ages and is one of the most common long-term conditions worldwide.

Symptoms of Asthma:
The most common symptoms include shortness of breath, wheezing (a whistling sound when breathing), chest tightness or pain, and persistent coughing especially at night or early morning. Symptoms can range from mild to severe and may vary from person to person.

Types of Asthma:
Allergic asthma is triggered by allergens such as pollen, dust mites, mold, and pet dander. Non-allergic asthma is triggered by factors like stress, exercise, cold air, smoke, or respiratory infections. Exercise-induced asthma causes symptoms during or after physical activity. Occupational asthma is caused by workplace irritants such as chemical fumes or dust.

Common Triggers:
Asthma triggers include allergens (pollen, dust mites, cockroaches, mold, pet dander), respiratory infections (colds, flu), air pollution, tobacco smoke, strong odors or chemical fumes, cold or dry air, physical exercise, stress and strong emotions, and certain medications like aspirin or beta-blockers.

Diagnosis:
Doctors diagnose asthma using spirometry (a breathing test measuring lung function), peak flow measurement, methacholine challenge test, allergy testing, and chest X-rays to rule out other conditions.

Treatment and Management:
Asthma has no cure but can be well-controlled with proper treatment. Quick-relief (rescue) inhalers such as albuterol are used during asthma attacks to relax airway muscles. Long-term control medications include inhaled corticosteroids (like fluticasone), long-acting beta-agonists, and leukotriene modifiers. Biologic therapies are available for severe asthma. An asthma action plan created with your doctor helps you know what to do when symptoms worsen.

When to Seek Emergency Care:
Call 911 or go to the emergency room immediately if you experience rapid worsening of shortness of breath, no improvement after using a rescue inhaler, shortness of breath even at rest, or bluish color to lips or fingernails (cyanosis).

Prevention:
Identify and avoid personal triggers, take medications as prescribed, monitor breathing with a peak flow meter, get vaccinated for flu and pneumonia, and maintain regular follow-up appointments with your doctor.
