First Aid and Emergency Response Guide

First aid is the immediate assistance given to a person suffering from illness or injury before professional medical help arrives. Knowing basic first aid can save lives.

Heart Attack - Recognition and Response:
Symptoms of a heart attack include chest pain or pressure, pain radiating to the arm jaw neck or back, shortness of breath, cold sweat, nausea, and lightheadedness. Call 911 immediately. Have the person sit or lie down in a comfortable position. If the person is not allergic to aspirin and can swallow, give them one regular aspirin (325mg) or four baby aspirins to chew. Loosen tight clothing. Begin CPR if the person becomes unresponsive and stops breathing normally.

Stroke - FAST Recognition:
Use the FAST acronym. Face drooping - ask the person to smile, does one side droop? Arm weakness - ask the person to raise both arms, does one drift downward? Speech difficulty - ask them to repeat a simple phrase, is their speech slurred or strange? Time to call 911. Note the time symptoms started as this affects treatment options. Do not give food or water as stroke can impair swallowing.

CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation):
For adults who are unresponsive and not breathing normally. Call 911 first or have someone else call while you begin CPR. Place the heel of your hand on the center of the chest. Place your other hand on top and interlace fingers. Push hard and fast at least 2 inches deep at a rate of 100 to 120 compressions per minute. If trained in CPR give 2 rescue breaths after every 30 compressions. Continue until professional help arrives or an AED becomes available.

Choking - Heimlich Maneuver:
For a conscious adult who is choking and cannot speak cough or breathe. Stand behind the person and wrap your arms around their waist. Make a fist and place it just above the navel. Grasp your fist with your other hand and give quick upward thrusts. Repeat until the object is expelled or the person becomes unconscious. For an unconscious person lower them to the floor and begin CPR.

Severe Bleeding:
Apply firm direct pressure to the wound using a clean cloth or bandage. Do not remove the cloth if it becomes soaked, add more on top. Maintain pressure for at least 15 minutes without peeking. For limb wounds a tourniquet may be needed if bleeding is life threatening. Elevate the injured area above heart level if possible. Call 911 for severe or uncontrolled bleeding.

Burns:
For minor burns cool the burn under cool (not cold) running water for 10 to 20 minutes. Do not use ice butter or toothpaste. Cover with a clean non-fluffy material. Take over-the-counter pain relief if needed. Seek medical care for burns larger than 3 inches, burns on face hands feet genitals or major joints, deep burns, chemical or electrical burns, and burns in young children or elderly people.

Allergic Reaction and Anaphylaxis:
Mild allergic reactions cause hives itching and mild swelling. Treat with antihistamines. Anaphylaxis is a severe life-threatening reaction causing throat swelling, difficulty breathing, rapid pulse, dizziness, and loss of consciousness. Use epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) immediately if available. Call 911 even after using epinephrine as symptoms can return. Lay the person flat with legs elevated unless they have breathing difficulty.

Fractures and Sprains:
Immobilize the injured area. Do not attempt to realign the bone. Apply ice wrapped in cloth for 20 minutes at a time to reduce swelling. Elevate if possible. Seek medical evaluation for suspected fractures. For sprains use the RICE method - Rest, Ice, Compression with a bandage, Elevation.

Fainting:
Lay the person on their back and elevate their legs 12 inches if no head neck or spine injury is suspected. Loosen tight clothing. Check for breathing. If they do not regain consciousness within one minute call 911.
